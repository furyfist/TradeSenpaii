# PART ONE: Version 1.0 — Foundation

## 1. What Is TradeSenpai and What Problem It Solves

TradeSenpai is an AI-powered stock research tool that combines historical price pattern analysis with SEC regulatory filing sentiment to generate next-day directional predictions and explainable research summaries for retail investors.

The problem it addresses is information asymmetry in retail investing. When a company files an 8-K with the SEC announcing a major acquisition, earnings restatement, or litigation event, institutional investors have teams of analysts who read these documents within minutes and act accordingly. A retail investor either never reads these filings or reads them days later after the market has already priced in the information.

TradeSenpai automates the research pipeline that institutional analysts run manually — downloading SEC filings, extracting meaningful financial language, scoring it for sentiment using domain-specific financial linguistics, and combining it with technical price analysis — into a single dashboard that updates in real time. The directional prediction is one output of this process, but the signal breakdown and sentiment history are arguably more valuable to a user making an informed decision.

The product is explicitly positioned as an educational simulation tool, not a trading system. The model achieves approximately 52% accuracy on next-day directional prediction, which is honest and realistic. Academic research consistently shows short-term stock price prediction rarely exceeds 55–56% on out-of-sample data. The value of TradeSenpai is not the raw accuracy number — it is the aggregation and visualization of signals that most retail investors never see.

## 2. How the Idea Evolved — Version 1

### Phase 1 — The Original Concept

The project started with a broad and ambitious stated goal: build an AI-powered stock advisor that analyzes real-time news sentiment and price patterns, matches current market conditions to historical scenarios, and predicts next-day movement with confidence. This framing had several implicit assumptions baked in that turned out to be either naive or impractical.

The initial concept imagined a case-based reasoning system where the model would say "this situation is similar to October 2008" and draw predictive conclusions from historical analogies. This was abandoned early because defining a robust similarity metric for financial time series is non-trivial, historical analogies rarely hold precisely enough to be actionable across different market regimes, and implementing it would have taken months before producing any usable output. The feature was eventually implemented in V2 as a supporting context layer rather than the primary prediction mechanism.

### Phase 2 — Narrowing to One Stock

The scope was narrowed to a single stock — Coca-Cola (KO) — for a deliberate reason. Generalizing across hundreds of stocks requires enormous data infrastructure, cross-stock normalization, sector adjustments, and model complexity that would have paralyzed the project. By committing to KO specifically, every decision could be optimized for one ticker. The dataset would be deep rather than wide. This was the correct call, and it became the template for how V2 expanded to additional tickers.

### Phase 3 — The News Data Problem

The original plan was to use general financial news APIs for the sentiment layer — Bloomberg, NewsAPI, or similar services. After exploring the options, SEC EDGAR filings were chosen instead. The reasoning was sound: SEC filings are structured, timestamped precisely, legally required, historically deep going back to 1993, and completely free to access. General financial news APIs are expensive at scale, noisy, and their sentiment is already partially priced into the market by the time you read it. SEC 8-K filings — particularly event-driven announcements like earnings, acquisitions, and legal settlements — represent raw information before it gets filtered through financial media.

### Phase 4 — From FinBERT to Loughran-McDonald

The sentiment model went through three iterations, each representing a meaningful pivot. The initial plan was to use FinBERT (ProsusAI/finbert), a BERT model fine-tuned on financial text, running on Kaggle's free GPU. After running inference on 462 KO filings, every single filing came back labeled "neutral" with near-identical confidence scores. Three alternative models were tried sequentially: `yiyanghkust/finbert-tone` collapsed to 100% positive, `mrm8488/distilroberta` collapsed to 100% negative, `nickmuchi/sec-bert` also collapsed to 100% negative. The pattern was identical across all models.

The root cause was a fundamental mismatch between what these models were trained on and what they were being fed. FinBERT and its variants were trained on financial news headlines and analyst reports — short, opinion-heavy sentences like "strong earnings beat expectations." SEC filings are written in formal legal language designed to be factually precise and legally defensible. After implementing a keyword filter that extracted only financially meaningful sentences before scoring, the distribution improved marginally but still showed overwhelming positive bias because Coca-Cola's filings are written by their communications team and are genuinely positive in tone regardless of underlying conditions.

The pivot to Loughran-McDonald (LM) was the correct architectural decision and should have been made first. The LM dictionary was built specifically from SEC filings — it was constructed by analyzing which words in 10-K documents predicted negative abnormal stock returns. It operates as a word-count scoring system rather than a neural classifier, making it deterministic, interpretable, fast, and free. The final LM scores showed genuine variance across 462 filings, ranging from -3.85 to +2.96. This was exactly the kind of signal the ML model needed.

## 3. Major Technical Decisions — Version 1

### 3.1 Why `sec_edgar_downloader` Over Raw Requests

The initial SEC data collection script used raw HTTP requests to the EDGAR API — approximately 200 lines of code handling pagination, rate limiting, document index parsing, and file downloading. After building it, the question was raised: why not use `sec_edgar_downloader`, a Python library that handles all of this? The library was strictly better for this use case — it reduced 200 lines to 20, handled rate limiting automatically, and had built-in resume capability. The original script was replaced.

### 3.2 The `sec_edgar_downloader` File Format Surprise

After downloading all filings, the indexing script failed because it was looking for `filing-details.xml` (which does not exist in the library's output) and `primary-document.html` (which existed for newer filings but not older ones). Older filings were saved as `primary-document` with no file extension at all. The fix required manually inspecting downloaded files to understand the actual structure. The real format was `full-submission.txt` — a concatenated bundle of all documents in the filing separated by `DOCUMENT` and `TYPE` tags in legacy SGML format dating back to the 1990s. The correct approach was to parse `DOCUMENT` blocks directly by matching the `TYPE` field.

### 3.3 Removing Leaky Features

The first training run used all available features including same-day open, high, low, close, and volume. These features represent information available during the trading day but not at the time a prediction would be made for the next day. Removing them did not dramatically change accuracy, which validated the decision — they were adding noise rather than signal because intraday price behavior correlates weakly with next-day direction over 30 years of data.

### 3.4 Model Selection — XGBoost vs LSTM vs Transformer

Three model types were trained and compared using TimeSeriesSplit cross-validation:

- **XGBoost**: 49.86% accuracy — below the 51.17% majority-class baseline
- **LSTM with bidirectional attention**: 52.50%
- **Transformer**: 52.72%
- **Ensemble of LSTM + Transformer**: 52.41% — worse than Transformer alone because including the underperforming XGBoost degraded the signal

XGBoost underperformed because it treats features independently and cannot model temporal dependencies between consecutive trading days. Stock prediction is fundamentally a sequential problem that tree-based ensembles cannot capture by design.

The final production model uses a Transformer with `d_model=128`, 4 attention heads, 3 encoder layers, pre-norm (`norm_first=True`), and a 60-day lookback sequence. This configuration was chosen to balance model complexity against the dataset size of approximately 7,889 training samples.

### 3.5 Accepting 52% Accuracy

52.72% accuracy on next-day stock direction prediction with no data leakage is the honest result. The decision was made to accept it and build the application rather than chase higher accuracy through increasingly complex methods that would likely overfit. The framing of the product was repositioned accordingly: not "predicts stocks" but "surfaces signals that institutional analysts use, automated for retail investors." This reframing is not spin — it is a more accurate description of what the system actually does and why it is genuinely useful.

## 4. System Architecture — Version 1

### 4.1 Data Pipeline

The V1 data pipeline consisted of five Python scripts running sequentially:

1. **`fetch_sec_filings.py`** — Downloaded all 8-K, 10-Q, and 10-K filings from SEC EDGAR for KO using CIK `0000021344`.
2. **`preprocess_filings.py`** — Extracted the primary `DOCUMENT` block from each filing's `full-submission.txt`, stripped HTML and boilerplate, and extracted MD&A sections.
3. **`lm_sentiment.py`** — Applied the Loughran-McDonald dictionary to compute 6 sentiment features per filing.
4. **`merge_datasets.py`** — Joined price data with sentiment data on date using forward-fill logic.
5. **`fetch_stock_data.py`** — Downloaded historical OHLCV data from Yahoo Finance and engineered 33 price features.

### 4.2 Feature Set — 56 Features

The final merged dataset contained 56 features per trading day:

- **Price features**: daily return, gap percentage, close lags at 1/5/10 days, moving averages at 7/20/50/200 days, volatility at 20/30 days, volume ratio, momentum at 5/10 days, Bollinger Band distances, and RSI-14
- **Calendar features**: day of week, month, and quarter
- **Market regime**: encoded as a binary above/below MA200
- **LM base features**: positive, negative, uncertain, litigious, and constraining word counts
- **LM normalized and derived features**: sentiment score, moving averages of sentiment, sentiment delta, uncertainty z-score, litigation spike flag, and negative dominance flag
- **Sentiment lags**: at 1/5/10 days
- **Return lags**: at 1/2/3/5 days
- **Regime indicator flags**
- **Interaction features**: combining sentiment with volatility and uncertainty

### 4.3 Backend — FastAPI

The FastAPI backend ran locally on port 8000 with five endpoints:

| Endpoint | Description |
|---|---|
| `GET /health` | Liveness checking |
| `GET /predict` | Main prediction with 30-minute in-memory caching |
| `GET /price-history` | 90-day OHLCV data |
| `GET /sentiment-history` | Last 50 LM sentiment data points |
| `GET /model-info` | Model metadata |

The 30-minute cache was implemented after discovering a race condition where simultaneous frontend requests could each trigger a fresh yfinance download, causing intermittent failures.

### 4.4 Frontend — React/Vite

The React frontend was built with Vite and consisted of four components:

- **`PredictionCard`** — Displaying direction and confidence
- **`SignalBreakdown`** — Showing 6 key signals with color-coded states
- **`PriceChart`** — Rendering a 90-day area chart with Recharts
- **`SentimentGauge`** — Showing LM sentiment history as a line chart

All styling was done with inline CSS objects. The frontend loaded all four API endpoints simultaneously using `Promise.all`.

## 5. Key Bugs and Failures — Version 1

### Bug 1 — EDGAR DNS Resolution Failure

During the first run of `fetch_sec_filings.py`, the script completed downloading 8-K and 10-Q filings but crashed during 10-K with `socket.gaierror: getaddrinfo failed`. The DNS resolution for `data.sec.gov` failed transiently. The fix was simply to re-run the script — the library's built-in skip logic detected already-downloaded filings and resumed. Root cause was a brief DNS failure on the local network, unrelated to the code.

### Bug 2 — `filing-details.xml` Does Not Exist

The original indexing code expected `sec_edgar_downloader` to save a `filing-details.xml` containing the filing date. This file does not exist in the library's output. The actual metadata was inside `full-submission.txt` with the format `FILED AS OF DATE: 20000126`. A regex fix extracted the date correctly.

### Bug 3 — Directory Index Pages Saved as Filing Content

For some filings, the `primary-document` file contained the HTML of the EDGAR directory listing page rather than the actual filing text. A junk detector was implemented that checked for fingerprint strings like `Parent Directory` and `global-search-form`. The real fix was switching to parsing `full-submission.txt` `DOCUMENT` blocks directly, which bypassed this problem entirely.

### Bug 4 — FinBERT Collapsing to Single Class

This was the most time-consuming problem in V1. Three separate FinBERT-family models were tested across multiple Kaggle notebook runs, each taking 15–20 minutes on GPU. All three collapsed to a single dominant class. The diagnosis required manually reading filing text files to confirm the content quality was not the issue — it was a model-data domain mismatch. The pivot to Loughran-McDonald resolved it permanently.

### Bug 5 — Missing Derived Sentiment Columns at Inference

When the FastAPI backend first called `load_latest_sentiment()`, it crashed with `KeyError: lm_sentiment_ma5`. The derived columns were computed inside `merge_datasets.py` and only saved to `merged_dataset.csv`. The raw `sec_sentiment_features.csv` did not contain them. The fix recomputed these columns on the fly inside `sentiment_loader.py` using identical rolling window logic.

### Bug 6 — Too Few Rows After `dropna()`

The inference pipeline fetched 300 calendar days of price history, then applied `dropna()` to remove NaN values from rolling features. The `ma_200` feature requires 200 rows of history before producing a valid value, leaving only approximately 10 clean rows. The Transformer needed 60 rows minimum. The fix increased the fetch window to 500 calendar days, yielding approximately 350 trading days and 150 clean rows after the warmup period.

### Bug 7 — Race Condition on `/predict`

The frontend fired all four API calls simultaneously using `Promise.all`. The `/predict` endpoint sometimes returned 500 during simultaneous calls because yfinance occasionally returned empty data when called rapidly in parallel. The fix added retry logic with up to 3 attempts and 2-second delays, plus 30-minute in-memory caching on the prediction response.

## 6. Biggest Lessons — Version 1

**Lesson 1 — Domain-specific tools beat general-purpose models**
Loughran-McDonald, a 2011 word list built specifically for SEC filings, outperformed three modern neural models trained on financial text. The reason is domain fit — LM was built from the exact document type being analyzed. When a domain-specific tool exists, evaluate it before reaching for a general-purpose neural model.

**Lesson 2 — Inspect your data before modeling**
The FinBERT collapse would have been diagnosed faster by manually reading 5–10 filing texts and asking whether the text looks like what FinBERT was trained on. The answer would have been obviously no — these are legal filings, not news articles. Data inspection before modeling saves more time than it costs.

**Lesson 3 — Training-inference consistency is the hardest production ML problem**
The missing derived columns bug was a textbook training-serving skew problem. Features computed during training were not available at inference time because they came from a downstream merge step rather than the raw data source. The fix — recomputing derived features from raw data at inference time — is the correct production ML pattern.

**Lesson 4 — Scope ruthlessly for V1**
Committing to a single stock, a single model architecture, a single sentiment method, and a simple binary target was the right call. Every expansion of scope multiplies complexity non-linearly. The product that shipped was complete, working, and honest.

**Lesson 5 — Positioning matters as much as accuracy**
A product positioned as "predicts stock prices" would be evaluated against that claim and found wanting at 52.72%. The same product positioned as "automates SEC filing research for retail investors" is genuinely valuable. How you frame the problem determines whether the solution is impressive or disappointing, even when the underlying system is identical.

# PART TWO: Version 2.0 — Multi-Ticker Expansion + Agentic Layer

## 7. What Changed in Version 2 and Why

Version 2 was motivated by two clear limitations of V1 that became obvious once the product was working. First, the tool covered only one stock — Coca-Cola — which made it a demonstration project rather than a usable product. Second, it produced predictions without explanation — users could see a DOWN prediction at 70% confidence but had no way to understand why, what historical precedents existed, or what risks could invalidate the call. V2 addressed both limitations simultaneously.

The guiding principle throughout V2 development was identical to V1: go deep rather than wide, reuse existing code wherever possible, and accept honest limitations rather than chasing impressive-sounding features. Every decision was filtered through this lens.

---

## 8. Multi-Ticker Expansion — Decisions and Reasoning

### 8.1 Why These Six Tickers

The initial proposed ticker universe included Coca-Cola (KO), Johnson & Johnson (JNJ), Procter & Gamble (PG), Walmart (WMT), GSK PLC (GSK), and Iberdrola (IBDRY). After analysis, GSK and Iberdrola were dropped and Apple (AAPL) and Alphabet (GOOGL) were added.

**GSK** was dropped because it is UK-headquartered and files with the SEC as a foreign private issuer using Form 20-F and Form 6-K instead of the standard domestic 10-K and 8-K formats. The preprocessing pipeline was written for domestic SGML submission formats and would require significant rework for foreign filer formats. GSK also underwent a major restructuring in 2022 by spinning off Haleon, creating a structural break in the data that would confuse any model trained on continuous history.

**Iberdrola** was dropped because it is a Spanish utility trading as an ADR on US markets. Its filings mix Spanish regulatory language with EU accounting standards, and the Loughran-McDonald dictionary was built from US corporate filings. LM would perform poorly on Iberdrola's filing language, making the sentiment signal unreliable.

**Apple and Alphabet** were added as technology sector representation to contrast with the defensive blue-chip stocks. Both have deep EDGAR filing histories, file standard domestic forms, and their financial language is well-covered by the LM dictionary.

The final six-ticker universe — **KO, JNJ, PG, WMT, AAPL, GOOGL** — spans consumer staples, healthcare, retail, and technology, providing meaningful sector diversity while maintaining pipeline reliability.

### 8.2 Separate Models Per Ticker vs Shared Model

The architectural decision of whether to train one model per ticker or a single shared model with ticker embeddings was resolved in favor of **separate models**. A shared model with ticker embeddings requires sufficient cross-ticker training data to generalize, introduces cross-ticker correlation noise where AAPL's volatility patterns could interfere with KO's defensive behavior, and makes debugging significantly harder when one ticker performs poorly. Six separate models using the identical V1 Transformer architecture is less elegant but more reliable and more faithful to the V1 principle of going deep on each ticker independently.

### 8.3 The Config File Decision

Before writing any new pipeline code, a central configuration file (`config.py`) was introduced as the single source of truth for all ticker metadata — CIK numbers, company names, sector classifications, and path helper functions. This was the **most important architectural decision of V2**. Without it, CIK numbers would have been hardcoded in multiple scripts, path conventions would have diverged between tickers, and adding a new ticker in V3 would require touching every script individually. With `config.py`, adding a new ticker requires changing exactly one file.

## 9. The Migration Problem — V1 to V2 Folder Structure

V1 had a flat folder structure where all processed files lived in `data/processed/` without ticker-specific subdirectories. V2 required a ticker-based structure where each ticker has its own subdirectory: `data/processed/KO/`, `data/processed/JNJ/`, and so on. This migration caused several days of debugging that could have been avoided with better planning.

### Migration Mistake 1 — Files Moved to Wrong Root

When manually migrating KO files from the flat V1 structure to the new ticker-based structure, files were moved to `TradeSenpai/data/processed/KO/` instead of `TradeSenpai/stock-analysis/data/processed/KO/`. The project has two nested directories — `TradeSenpai/` as the monorepo root containing both `app/` and `stock-analysis/` subdirectories. The data belongs inside `stock-analysis/data/`, not at the monorepo root level. The mistake was caught by running `lm_sentiment.py --ticker KO` and observing a `FileNotFoundError` pointing to the correct expected path.

### Migration Mistake 2 — Wrong File Name

The original KO price file was named `coca_cola_cleaned.csv` in the V1 flat structure. The new `config.py` expected it at `data/processed/KO/cleaned.csv`. After moving the file, `merge_datasets.py` still failed because the file that was moved was actually the raw price data with capitalized column names (`Date`, `Close`) and DD-MM-YYYY date format — not the fully engineered feature file with 33 columns. The fully processed file had been lost during migration. The solution was to create `fetch_stock_data.py` from scratch, a script that had never existed in V1 because the KO price data had been processed manually before the project started. This script downloads fresh data from yfinance and engineers all price features, and became the foundation for processing all new tickers.

### Migration Mistake 3 — `fetch_sec_filings.py` Had `download_all_filings()` Commented Out

When running the pipeline for JNJ, the `fetch_sec_filings.py` script produced an empty `filings_index.csv`. Examining the code revealed that the `download_all_filings()` call was commented out in the main block — it had been commented out during V1 development after the initial KO download and never uncommented. Additionally, the `COMPANY_NAME` variable was referenced inside the download function but never defined at the module level. Both issues were fixed: `COMPANY_NAME` was added to the config block after the argparse section, and the download call was uncommented.

## 11. Multi-Ticker Backend Bugs

### Bug 1 — `load_latest_sentiment()` Missing Ticker Argument

After updating all backend files for multi-ticker support, the first `/predict` call failed with `TypeError: load_latest_sentiment() missing 1 required positional argument: 'ticker'`. The `feature_engineer.py` file was calling `load_latest_sentiment()` without passing the ticker variable. A simple one-line fix.

### Bug 2 — PyTorch 2.6 `weights_only` Default Change

After fixing the sentiment argument, all `/predict` calls failed with `_pickle.UnpicklingError`. PyTorch 2.6 changed the default value of the `weights_only` argument in `torch.load` from `False` to `True`. The fix was adding `weights_only=False` to the `torch.load` call in `predictor.py`. This is acceptable because the model files are generated by the project's own training notebook and are trusted sources.

### Bug 3 — Hardcoded KO in `get_latest_feature_row()`

After fixing the PyTorch loading error, GOOGL and other tickers still failed with `ValueError: Need at least 60 rows, got 0`. Debug output showed the `feature_df` arriving at `predictor.py` with 0 rows despite `engineer_features()` producing 67 rows correctly. The root cause was that `get_latest_feature_row()` was calling `fetch_recent_prices('KO')` with a hardcoded string instead of using the ticker variable. Every ticker prediction was downloading KO price data, then trying to match KO prices with GOOGL sentiment, producing all-NaN features that collapsed to 0 rows after `dropna()`. The fix was using the ticker parameter consistently throughout the function.

### Bug 4 — yfinance Returning Only 67 Rows

After fixing the hardcoded KO bug, predictions still failed with 0 rows. The fetch window of 500 calendar days was only returning 67 rows from yfinance for some tickers — insufficient for the `ma_200` warmup requirement of 200 rows. The fetch window was increased to 800 calendar days, yielding sufficient clean rows after the warmup period.

## 12. The Agentic Explanation Layer

### 12.1 Why an Agentic Layer

A prediction alone — "KO: DOWN at 74.7% confidence" — tells a retail investor nothing actionable. They cannot see why the model made this call, whether similar historical situations supported or contradicted it, or what risks could make the prediction wrong. The agentic layer transforms TradeSenpai from a black-box predictor into an explainable research tool. This is the feature that most directly addresses the information asymmetry problem the product was built to solve.

### 12.2 Historical Similarity Search

The similarity engine uses cosine similarity to find the three most similar historical trading days to the current day from each ticker's `merged_dataset.csv`. Every trading day in the dataset is a vector of the same 38 features used for similarity comparison — price features, momentum, volatility, RSI, and LM sentiment features. The current day's feature vector is compared against every historical day using cosine similarity, which measures the angle between vectors rather than their magnitude. This makes it robust to scale differences between features.

An important design decision was filtering out the last 365 days from the analogy pool. Returning a "similar day was 3 months ago" as a historical analogy adds no context — it is too recent to have meaningful news attached to it. The 365-day minimum age ensures all analogies are genuinely historical with sufficient time for news events to be well-documented.

The similarity search for KO on February 2026 returned three analogies:

| Date | Similarity | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| March 28, 2023 | 96.8% | UP +0.72% |
| April 4, 2023 | 96.3% | UP +0.95% |
| February 10, 2025 | 96.1% | UP +4.72% |

All three analogies resulted in UP despite the model predicting DOWN at 74.7% confidence. This tension — similar historical conditions led to UP outcomes but the current model predicts DOWN — is exactly the kind of nuance the explanation layer is designed to surface.

### 12.3 Groq LLM Integration

The LLM synthesis layer uses Groq's free API with the Llama 3.1 8B Instant model. Groq was chosen over OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google because it offers a genuinely free tier with no credit card requirement and extremely fast inference at approximately 200 tokens per second. The model is smaller than GPT-4 or Claude Sonnet, but for the task of synthesizing structured quantitative signals into plain English, it performs adequately.

The prompt engineering approach was deliberate. The LLM is given the current prediction, confidence, top signals, and formatted historical analogies, and asked to produce a structured JSON response with six fields: `headline`, `explanation`, `key_driver`, `main_risk`, `historical_note`, and `confidence_tier`. The temperature was set to 0.3 to prioritize factual accuracy over creative language. The prompt explicitly prohibits buy/sell recommendations and requires honest acknowledgment of the 52% accuracy limitation.

The LLM output for KO produced a coherent explanation that correctly identified the tension between the model's DOWN prediction and the historical analogies showing UP outcomes. It noted the neutral RSI reading of 68.75 as insufficient to confirm a strong upward trend, identified the positive SEC sentiment score of 0.6369 as a counterbalancing signal, and correctly flagged a sudden increase in positive sentiment as the main risk that could invalidate the prediction. The explanation demonstrated genuine synthesis of the quantitative signals rather than generic financial commentary.

### 12.4 Confidence Tier Classification

Raw confidence scores like 0.7029 are not intuitive for retail investors. The confidence tier system translates the raw probability into four human-readable categories:

| Tier | Threshold |
|---|---|
| Low Signal | Below 55% |
| Moderate Signal | 55–65% |
| Strong Signal | 65–75% |
| High Conviction | Above 75% |

This makes the uncertainty visible and prevents users from over-interpreting a 52% model that happens to output 70% confidence on a particular day.

## 13. Frontend Evolution — V1 to V2

The V1 frontend was a single-ticker dashboard hardcoded to KO. Several components had company names hardcoded as text strings rather than reading from the API response. The V2 frontend required three main additions: a ticker selector bar, dynamic data loading per ticker, and the AI Explanation Panel.

The ticker selector was implemented as a horizontal button bar below the header, with each button showing the ticker symbol and company name. Clicking a ticker clears the current prediction, sets a loading state, and fires all four API calls simultaneously for the new ticker. The active ticker is highlighted with an indigo border and background.

A subtle UX improvement was separating the loading of fast endpoints from the slow prediction endpoint. Previously, `Promise.all` waited for all four endpoints before rendering anything. The revised approach loads price history, sentiment history, and model info first — these return in under one second — and displays the charts immediately. The prediction card shows a loading state while `/predict` completes its yfinance download and model inference. This reduces perceived load time significantly.

The AI Explanation Panel is collapsed by default to avoid slowing the initial page load. Expanding it triggers the `/explain` API call, which takes 3–5 seconds to complete the similarity search and LLM synthesis. The panel shows the headline, detailed explanation, key driver, main risk boxes, three historical analogy cards with similarity progress bars, and a disclaimer. Each analogy card shows the outcome direction color-coded in green or red, the actual return percentage, similarity score, and the top 3 key signals from that historical day.

## 14. What Was Removed, Simplified, or Not Built

- **Google Search enrichment** — planned but not yet implemented. The original vision was to pass historical analogy dates to a Groq web search tool call to retrieve actual news from those dates. This would transform "March 2023, UP +0.72%" into "March 2023, Coca-Cola Q4 earnings beat organic revenue growth of 7%, driving the gain." Deferred to V2.1 because Groq's compound-beta model with built-in web search was still experimental at time of writing.

- **Six-level agentic architecture** — simplified to three functional layers: quantitative signal extraction, historical similarity search, and LLM synthesis. The original specification proposed six separate agents for hypothesis generation, context extraction, prediction, contradiction analysis, synthesis, and alert generation. This was correctly identified as over-engineering for a demo product.

- **Prediction history logging** — no database stores historical predictions and their actual outcomes. The accuracy displayed is CV training accuracy, not a live track record. A PostgreSQL table logging each prediction with its actual next-day outcome would make the product significantly more trustworthy but was deferred as infrastructure work.

- **Earnings calendar flag** — the model treats earnings days identically to ordinary trading days despite earnings announcements being the highest-volatility events in a stock's calendar. A binary flag for the 5 days before and after quarterly earnings would improve signal quality. One-line addition to feature engineering that was never implemented.

- **Mobile responsive layout** — the dashboard uses a fixed two-column grid that breaks below approximately 800px screen width. Not implemented because the primary use case is desktop research.

## 15. Current State of the Project — Version 2.0

Version 2.0 is a fully functional end-to-end system covering six tickers across three sectors. The data pipeline processes 7,888–7,889 trading days per ticker with 48 base features and 8 additional engineered features. Six separate Transformer models achieve 51.9–53.5% CV accuracy on next-day direction classification with no data leakage. The FastAPI backend serves live predictions by fetching real-time price data and combining it with historical LM sentiment. The React dashboard displays per-ticker predictions, confidence scores, 90-day price charts, SEC sentiment history, signal breakdowns, and AI-generated explanations with historical analogies.

The system correctly handles all six tickers through a unified pipeline parameterized by ticker symbol. Switching tickers in the frontend loads the corresponding model, sentiment data, and price history within 3–8 seconds depending on network conditions. The explanation layer generates coherent, factually grounded narratives that reference specific signal values and historical dates rather than producing generic financial commentary.

## 16. Known Limitations — Version 2.0

- **52% accuracy** — all six models hover near the 51–54% range. This reflects the fundamental difficulty of next-day stock price prediction. The honest response is to present it clearly rather than hide it.
- **Forward-filled sentiment** — LM sentiment from the most recent filing is applied to every subsequent trading day until the next filing. A 10-Q filed in November persists through February. The assumption is defensible but imperfect.
- **No earnings calendar awareness** — the five days around quarterly earnings are the highest-volatility events in a stock's calendar and are treated identically to ordinary days.
- **No macro features** — VIX, S&P500 correlation, interest rates, and dollar index are excluded. All six stocks have significant correlation with broader market conditions that the model ignores.
- **yfinance dependency** — real-time price data relies on Yahoo Finance with no SLA, rate limits, and occasional data gaps. A production system would use a paid data provider.
- **Local deployment only** — the application runs locally with no cloud deployment, authentication, or rate limiting.
- **No Google Search enrichment** — historical analogy context comes from the LLM's training knowledge rather than live news retrieval.
- **Class collapse on some folds** — KO, PG, and AAPL models showed fold-specific collapse to predicting a single class. The CV scores remain valid but indicate these models are operating near the noise floor of predictability for those tickers.

## 17. Technical Debt — Version 2.0

- **Inline CSS throughout React components** — all styling uses JavaScript style objects. A proper CSS framework or styled components would make theming and maintenance significantly easier.
- **No test coverage anywhere** — zero unit tests, integration tests, or end-to-end tests in either the backend or frontend.
- **Hardcoded relative paths in backend** — `sentiment_loader.py` and `similarity_search.py` use `../../stock-analysis/data/processed/` which is fragile and breaks if the directory structure changes.
- **`GROQ_API_KEY` hardcoded in `explainer.py`** — should be an environment variable loaded from a `.env` file.
- **No persistent prediction logging** — predictions are generated and forgotten. There is no mechanism to evaluate real-world accuracy over time.
- **`preprocess_filings.py` MD&A extraction uses brittle regex** — approximate pattern matching that may fail on unusual filing formats.
- **The LM dictionary in `lm_sentiment.py` is a manually curated subset** — the full Loughran-McDonald master dictionary contains over 86,000 word classifications and would improve coverage.
- **`fetch_stock_data.py` and `feature_engineer.py` both compute price features independently** — a shared feature computation module would eliminate the duplication and class of bugs it causes.

## 18. What We Would Do Differently If Rebuilding Today

**Start with a shared feature module**
The biggest source of bugs across both versions was training-serving skew — features computed differently in the training pipeline versus the inference pipeline. Building a single shared module that both training notebooks and the inference backend import from would eliminate this entire class of bugs. The module would be the contract that both sides must satisfy.

**Start with Loughran-McDonald**
The FinBERT experimentation consumed the most time in V1 and produced zero usable output. LM was the correct tool from the beginning and should have been the first choice, not the fallback after multiple failed neural model attempts.

**Design the folder structure before writing code**
The V1-to-V2 migration required manually moving files and debugging path errors for multiple sessions. A well-designed ticker-parameterized folder structure from day one — even for a single ticker — would have made V2 expansion trivial rather than error-prone.

**Predict 5-day returns instead of next-day direction**
Next-day prediction at 52% accuracy is marginally better than chance. Five-day return prediction would capture more of the sentiment-driven signal because SEC filing effects play out over multiple days rather than in a single session. The target variable choice fundamentally caps achievable accuracy.

**Add the earnings calendar flag from day one**
This is a one-line addition to feature engineering that was never implemented despite being one of the most important features for any earnings-driven stock. The five days before and after quarterly earnings are the highest-signal events in the dataset.

**Use environment variables from day one**
The Groq API key ended up hardcoded in `explainer.py`. Any credential hardcoded in source code is a security risk and a deployment problem. A `.env` file with `python-dotenv` should have been the default from the first line of backend code.

## 19. Biggest Lessons — Version 2

**Lesson 1 — One config file prevents hundreds of bugs**
`config.py` with CIK numbers, path helpers, and ticker metadata was the single most valuable addition in V2. Every pipeline script that previously had hardcoded paths or ticker values became parameterized with two lines of change. The hardcoded KO bug in `get_latest_feature_row()` — where all six tickers were downloading KO price data — would have been caught immediately if the function had been written with proper parameterization from the start.

**Lesson 2 — Migrate first, verify, then expand**
The correct V2 approach was: migrate KO to the new folder structure, verify KO still works end-to-end, then add new tickers. The actual approach involved debugging the migration and adding new tickers simultaneously, which made it harder to distinguish migration bugs from new-ticker bugs. Always complete the migration and verify the baseline before expanding scope.

**Lesson 3 — Debug output is not optional**
The hardcoded KO bug was invisible until debug print statements showed `[INFO] Fetching latest KO prices` appearing in the log for a GOOGL request. Without that print statement, the bug would have taken much longer to find. Meaningful log output that includes the ticker variable is not a nice-to-have in a multi-ticker system — it is essential for debugging.

**Lesson 4 — Three agents beat six theoretical ones**
The original agentic architecture specification proposed six separate agents with distinct responsibilities. In practice, three functional layers — quantitative extraction, historical similarity, and LLM synthesis — achieve the same explanatory goal with less complexity, lower latency, and fewer failure points. Over-specified architectures are a form of premature optimization.

**Lesson 5 — The explanation layer is more valuable than the prediction**
The AI explanation panel showing historical analogies, key drivers, and risk factors generated more interest and insight than the raw UP/DOWN prediction. A user who sees "model predicts DOWN but all three historical analogies resulted in UP" has more actionable information than a user who sees "DOWN at 74.7%." The quantitative prediction is the entry point; the explanation is the product.

**Lesson 6 — Cosine similarity on financial feature vectors works**
Finding the three most similar historical days using cosine similarity on 38 normalized features produced meaningful results on the first run — 96% similarity scores with genuinely similar RSI, sentiment, and momentum conditions. The simplicity of the approach relative to its output quality is striking. A 20-line numpy function replaced what could have been a complex vector database integration.
